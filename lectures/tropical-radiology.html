Introduction

Tropical diseases are diseases commoner in the tropics or transported to temperate regions by travelers.

The radiological diagnosis is all-encompassing, and the inherent immunity of the patient plays a large role in clinical presentation.

Soft Tissue & Skeletal System

Tropical Ulcer

Ainhum

Ainhum is a rare condition that primarily affects people of African descent. It involves the spontaneous development of a painful constriction ring, usually on the fifth toe.

Leprosy

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium leprae. It primarily affects the skin and nerves and can lead to disfigurement if left untreated.

Mycetoma

Mycetoma is a chronic, granulomatous infection that can affect the skin, subcutaneous tissue, and even bones. It is characterized by the formation of nodules and the presence of grains or fungal masses.

Mycetoma

Mycetoma is a fungal implantation that primarily occurs in bare feet but can also affect the skull and knee. It is caused by a fungus known as M. mycetoma, which infiltrates soft tissues, erodes the bone cortices, and induces cystic changes in the medullary cavity.

This process results in a characteristic appearance known as "madura foot," characterized by a shaggy periostitis, bony resorption, destruction, and reactive sclerosis. The affected area may resemble the appearance of melting snow.

Mycetoma cases often involve superimposed infections.

GIT-Amoebiasis

Gastrointestinal infestation with amoebiasis can affect various parts of the body:

  • Colon - colonic amoebiasis
  • Liver - amoebic liver disease
  • Chest - amoebic chest disease

Colonic Amoebiasis

Colonic amoebiasis can be diagnosed using barium studies, computed tomography, and ultrasound. Radiological features include:

  • Skip lesions
  • Hazy mucosal appearance
  • Spiculation due to contrast in the submucosal ulcers
  • Narrowing of the lumen
  • Ahaustral appearance

Fistula formations and amoebomas, which are localized hypertrophied granulomas, are also observed (Differential Diagnosis - carcinoma).

Amoebic Liver Abscess

Amoebic liver abscess initially appears as an ill-defined iso or hypoechoic lesion. It is typically located subcapsularly, more commonly in the right lobe than the left, and can be multiple. As the abscess progresses, it assumes a round or oval shape with a hypoechoic interior containing internal debris. It is surrounded by echogenic compressed liver tissue or anechoic edematous halo.

Differential diagnoses for amoebic liver disease include pyogenic abscess, hepatoma, infected hepatic cyst, and metastasis.

Amoebic Chest Disease

Amoebic chest disease can manifest with several features on a plain chest X-ray, which may include:

  • Pleural effusion (sympathetic)
  • Basal pneumonia
  • Empyema
  • Amoebic lung abscess

Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle Cell Disease is a genetic disorder caused by inherited hemoglobin S in the formation of red blood cells. It can manifest as a homozygous (SS) or heterozygous combination.

Radiological Features in Children

Hand & Foot Syndrome:
This condition can be observed as early as 6 months of age and is characterized by the following radiological features:

  • Swelling of the soft tissue around the digits
  • Widened intra-trabecular spaces with thinning of the trabecular (coarsened bony trabecular pattern)
  • Expansion of the shafts of the bones
  • Subperiosteal new bone formation

Radiological Features in Adults

  • Shortening of the digits may be observed due to previous dactylitis or elongation.
  • The bony trabecular pattern appears coarsened.
  • The central defect in the metaphyseal plates, resulting from previous infarction, gives rise to the "peg-in-hole" appearance, which is attributed to the dual blood supply.

Changes in Long Bones

Due to repeated infarcts in long bones, the following radiological features may be present:

  • Areas of radiolucency with subperiosteal new bone formation, leading to the "bone-in-bone" appearance.
  • Predisposition to salmonella osteomyelitis (usually diaphyseal), resulting in features like cortical sclerosis, loss of cortical medullary differentiation (CMD), sequestrum formation, bone-in-bone appearance, and cloaca formation seen as a lucent tract, often accompanied by pathological fractures.

Spinal Changes

  • Coarsened trabecular pattern.
  • Osteoporosis, seen as a reduction in bone density.
  • Flattened vertebral bodies with reduced heights and increased transverse diameter.
  • Concave indentation of the mid-aspects of the vertebral bodies, giving a "cod-fish" appearance.
  • Features of osteomyelitis.

Pelvis Changes

The changes in the pelvis are usually pathognomonic in SCD patients and are seen in the central portion of the pelvis. They include:

  • Coarsening of trabecular patterns with interlacing of sclerotic trabeculae interspersed by stippled calcific densities due to previous infarcts.

Chest Radiograph

Findings on chest radiographs in SCD patients may include:

  • Widened ribs due to marrow hyperplasia, showing coarsened trabecular patterns.
  • In older patients, the ribs and clavicles could be sclerotic.
  • General reduction in the lucency of the lungs due to repeated small infarcts, with increased susceptibility to lung infections that can manifest as consolidations.

Joints Changes

SCD patients are prone to avascular necrosis, which is more common in the hips and shoulder joints. Joint changes may include:

  • Flattened femoral/humeral head.
  • Patchy lucent and sclerotic areas.
  • Narrowed joint space and deformities.

Skull Changes

  • Widened diploic space with thinning of the cortex.
  • Granular appearance of the vault.
  • Hair-on-end appearance due to a vertical arrangement of the coarsened trabecular pattern. Other differentials include thalassemias, iron deficiency anemia, and rickets.

Extra-osseous Changes

Cardiovascular System (CVS)
  • Pan-cardiac chamber enlargement.
  • Increased broncho-vascular markings.
Renal System

In the renal system, the following changes may be observed:

  • Contrast study (IVU) shows blunting of the calyces due to renal papillary necrosis.
  • Pooling of contrast in the medulla due to infarcted tissue.
Liver

Liver-related changes in SCD include:

  • On plain abdominal x-ray: an increase in the hepatic shadow.
  • On ultrasound (USS), areas of infarcts seen as echogenicities, along with hepatomegaly.
  • The gallbladder may contain stones.
Spleen

In the spleen, SCD patients may exhibit:

  • Splenomegaly.
  • Abscess formation.

Schistosomiasis

  • Caused by the trematodes.
  • Four important species:
    • Schistosoma haematobium.
    • Schistosoma mansoni.
    • Schistosoma japonicum.
    • Schistosoma intercalatum.
  • Schistosomiasis haematobium mainly affects the urinary tract.
  • First-line radiological diagnosis is via intravenous urography (IVU), others are USS, plain radiography.
  • At the initial stage, thereâ€™s persistent filling of the lower ureters which later becomes dilated due to constriction within the bladder wall.
  • Thereâ€™s nodularity, beaded or irregularly dilated ureters.
  • Dilatation progresses and may affect the whole ureter bilaterally but usually asymmetrically.
  • Calcification of the ureters.
  • Affectation of the bladder better investigated by a cystogram:
    • Mucosal haziness (due to edema).
    • Multiple flat papillomas.
    • Thickened wall.
    • Calcification.
    • Reflux of contrast.
    • Upper tract dilatation as obstruction progresses.
  • Differential diagnosis (DD) includes renal tuberculosis, but this affects the upper renal tract at its initial stage.

Guinea Worm (Dracunculiasis) and Cysticercosis

  • Guinea worm infestation (Dracunculiasis) is endemic in developing countries, and the parasite gives string-like calcifications in the muscle.
  • Cysticercosis (Taenia Solium) infestation can affect any organ in the body, but the commonest organ is the brain.
  • The radiological feature in the muscle is elongated, rounded calcifications.
  • In the brain, the radiological features depend on the stage of the parasite:
    • It ranges from asymptomatic features to cysts (vesicles), edema, mural nodules, and calcifications.
    • They are better demonstrated using MRI.
    • The cysts usually show avid enhancement post-contrast injection with significant perilesional edema.

Hydatid Disease (Echinococcus)

  • An ancient disease with humans acting as the intermediate host.
  • Usually, cysts are demonstrable in the liver but could also affect the lungs, bone, and brain.
  • These cysts are seen as anechoic, usually well-circumscribed lesions with or without a floating membrane depending on the stage.
  • They may contain tiny echoes (sand) representing scolices.
  • On CT, cysts are seen as single, large, well-circumscribed hypodense lesions with no significant edema or ring enhancement post-contrast injection.
  • Ruptured daughter cysts can develop and are seen as strands within them.
