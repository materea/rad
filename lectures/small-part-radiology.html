Introduction

The small parts include:

  • Orbits and the eyes
  • Thyroid gland
  • Breast
  • Scrotal sac and testes

Ultrasound of Small Parts

In performing ultrasound scans of the small parts, high-frequency linear transducers are used.

The frequency is usually 7.5MHz and above.

Real-time B-scanning is the main technique, however, A-scanning may be used to demonstrate amplitude of echoes.

Doppler mode can also be put into use to assess blood flow patterns.

The Orbit

  • The orbit is the bony compartment of the globe within the skull.
  • Its pathology is of clinical importance because of the high value placed on vision.
  • Radiological modalities for imaging the orbit include:
    • Plain X-ray
    • Computed tomography
    • Magnetic resonance imaging
    • Radionuclide studies
    • Fluoroscopy
  • In our environment, the first-line investigation method for orbital trauma is used to evaluate fractures and foreign bodies.
  • Views include PA skull, lateral, and half-axial (Towneâ€™s) view.
  • Contrast study, such as Dacryocystography, is used to assess the lacrimal apparatus.
  • CT provides axial thin slices of about 2-3mm thickness and coronal slices of about 5mm, demonstrating bony and soft tissue abnormalities.
  • MRI is best for demonstrating pathology involving the nerves.

Orbital Trauma

  • Blow-out fractures:
    • Direct force resulting in fracture of the inferior floor or medial wall with decompression
    • Radiographic findings:
      • Fractures of floor and medial wall
      • Opacification of ethmoidal and maxillary sinuses
      • Herniation of extraocular muscles (possible)
      • Leakage of air into globe (intraorbital emphysema, possible)
  • Tripod fracture:
    • Fractures of the zygomatic arch, floor of the orbit, lateral maxillary sinus wall & zygomatico-frontal process
  • Le Fort type 1 & 2 fractures:
    • Involving the pterygoid plates, nasal cavity, and orbital walls
  • Fracture of the base of the skull with extension through the optic canal

Fractures can be shown on plain X-ray, CT, MRI.

Orbital Pseudotumor

Orbital pseudotumor is an idiopathic inflammatory condition that can affect the orbit and globe. It is frequently unilateral but could be bilateral and may present as a diffuse or focal condition.

Radiological findings:

  • CT scan shows a poorly defined, heterogeneous, hyperdense mass in the affected tissue.
  • MRI reveals hypointensity on both T1 and T2 sequences.

Differential Diagnosis (DD): Graveâ€™s Ophthalmopathy

It's important to note that a rapid response to steroid treatment aids in the diagnosis of orbital pseudotumor.

Graveâ€™s Ophthalmopathy

Graveâ€™s Ophthalmopathy is often seen as a result of Graveâ€™s disease, characterized by the enlargement of the extraocular muscles. It typically affects the inferior, medial, and superior recti muscles with the least affectation of the lateral rectus. The tendinous attachment of these muscles is usually spared, unlike in pseudotumor.

Radiological findings:

  • CT scan: Shows isodense enlarged muscles.
  • MRI: T2-weighted imaging will reveal hyperintense areas.

Rhabdomyosarcoma

Rhabdomyosarcoma is the most common primary orbital tumor in children. Approximately half of cases are metastases to the orbit, while the other half originate primarily from the orbit.

Radiological findings:

  • CT scan: Reveals isodense enlargement of extraocular muscles.
  • MRI: Shows isointense muscle mass.

Pathologies of the Globe

Lens Dislocation

Lens dislocation is a condition where the eye's lens shifts from its normal position. This condition may require medical attention.

Enucleation

Enucleation is the surgical removal of the eye. It is a procedure often performed when there are severe eye diseases or injuries that cannot be treated through other means.

Hemorrhage

Hemorrhage in the eye can result from various causes and may involve bleeding into the anterior or posterior chamber of the eye. It is a critical condition that needs prompt evaluation and management.

Retinal/Choroidal Detachment

Retinal detachment is a condition where the retina detaches from its normal position. It can be seen on ultrasound as a funnel-shaped echogenic strand attached to the optic nerve head posteriorly and ora serrata anteriorly. Retinal detachment can be complete or incomplete.

Choroid detachment appears as a convex indentation on the globe, typically laterally. Its anterior margin is attached to the ciliary body, and the posterior end is connected to the exit foramina of the vortex vein.

Optic Nerve Damage

Optic nerve damage refers to injuries or conditions affecting the optic nerve. The optic nerve is crucial for vision, and damage to it can result in vision problems.

Melanoma

Melanomas are malignant tumors that arise from the choroid layer of the globe. They are among the top 10 ocular malignancies in adults and are known for their high invasiveness.

Ultrasound (USS) findings:

  • Elevated homogenous echogenic focus, often referred to as a 'collar button,' along the posterior wall of the vitreous chamber.
  • Melanomas can also be diffuse (en plaque) or affect the entire globe.
  • Haemorrhagic retinal detachment, seen as a V-shaped hyperechoic area.
  • Tiny echogenic foci representing calcifications, although uncommonly seen.
  • Doppler ultrasound may reveal a low-resistance flow pattern.

CT (Computed Tomography):

  • Melanomas appear as homogeneously soft tissue density masses that extend into the vitreous cavity.
  • These masses typically show moderate contrast uptake.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging):

  • Melanomas are hyperintense on MRI due to the melanin component and exhibit marked enhancement after contrast administration.

Retinoblastoma

Retinoblastoma is the most common intraocular tumor in children. Approximately one-third of cases are bilateral and associated with an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern. It is considered a highly aggressive malignancy, typically classified as neuroectodermal tumors, and has a high recurrence rate.

Ultrasound (USS) findings:

  • Retinoblastoma appears as a highly echogenic mass with posterior shadowing in the posterior globe.
  • Doppler ultrasound may reveal an increase in blood flow within the mass.

CT (Computed Tomography):

  • CT imaging readily shows calcifications associated with retinoblastomas in up to 90% of cases.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging):

  • On T1-weighted images, retinoblastoma is slightly hyperintense compared to the extraocular muscles.
  • On T2-weighted images, there is increased signal intensity within the mass.
  • Gradient echo (GE) imaging highlights the calcifications as areas of hypointensity.
  • Retinoblastoma exhibits marked contrast uptake in the non-calcified portions of the mass.
  • MRI is especially useful for demonstrating trans-scleral or perineural extension and involvement of the pineal gland.

Optic Neuritis

Optic Neuritis is characterized by acute inflammation of the optic nerve and can occur either in isolation or in association with multiple sclerosis.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging):

  • On T2-weighted images (T2-W), areas of edema and fluid accumulation may be visible.
  • On post-contrast T1-weighted images (Post-Gd T1-W), there may be focal uptake at the sites corresponding to the areas of increased signal intensity on T2-W imaging.

The Thyroid Gland

Radiological investigations to image the thyroid gland include:

  • USS (Ultrasound) of the neck: Performed with a high-frequency, linear probe.
  • Plain X-ray of the neck
  • CT (Computed Tomography)
  • Radionuclide studies

Thyroid Ultrasound:

  • Performed with a high-frequency, linear probe.
  • Scans can be conducted in both transverse and longitudinal planes.
  • The thyroid gland appears as a uniform soft tissue echogenic structure.
  • The medial border is bounded by the isthmus lobe, while the posterior-lateral border is adjacent to the carotid sheath.
  • The normal gland measures approximately 5cm x 3cm x 2cm in length (sagittal), width, and thickness (transverse), respectively.

Goitre

Multinodular Colloid Goitre:

  • Occurs due to the accumulation of colloid within thyroid cells, which later coalesce and undergo necrosis, forming solid colloid nodules.
  • Over time, fibrosis, scarring, and calcification develop.

Radiological Findings:

  • The gland appears enlarged with poorly echogenic nodules.
  • The gland has a lobular contour.
  • Fibrosis is seen as foci of high echogenicity, while cysts and degeneration appear as hypoechoic areas.

Thyroid Adenoma

Thyroid Adenomas:

  • Thyroid adenomas are quite common, occurring in both sexes and at all ages.

Radiological Findings (USS):

  • Usually appears as an echogenic lesion with a characteristic thin, smooth halo around it.
  • Can show areas of cystic, fibrotic, or hemorrhagic degeneration.
  • Rarely multiple but can reach enormous size.

Thyroid Carcinoma

Thyroid Carcinoma:

  • Thyroid carcinoma includes different histological types:
    • Anaplastic (Rare but aggressive)
    • Papillary (commonest & usually with psammoma bodies)
    • Follicular
    • Medullary

Radiological Findings (USS):

  • Seen as a poorly echogenic, ill-defined mass.
  • May have calcifications (micro/coarse).
  • May show evidence of invasion of stroma and capsule.

Value of Plain Radiograph

Plain X-ray of the neck can provide valuable information, including:

  • Soft tissue swelling/mass: Detecting abnormalities in the soft tissues of the neck.
  • Areas of lucencies: Identifying regions with reduced density or transparency.
  • Areas of calcification: Highlighting the presence of calcified structures.
  • Tracheal deviation and compression: Revealing any displacement or pressure on the trachea.
  • Retrosternal extension: Indicating whether the condition extends behind the sternum.

Value of CT

CT (Computed Tomography) has several advantages in evaluating thyroid gland conditions:

  • Thyroid gland visualization: CT is effective in depicting the thyroid gland due to its high iodine content, making it appear relatively hyperdense on scans.
  • Detection of masses: CT can identify adenomas and carcinomas as soft tissue masses within the thyroid gland.
  • Limitations in differentiation: While CT is valuable for visualization and detecting abnormalities, it may not reliably differentiate between benign and malignant lesions.

The Breast

Various imaging modalities are used for breast assessment:

  • X-ray mammography:
    • Screen-film mammography
    • Digital mammography
  • Breast USS (Ultrasound):
    • Real-time ultrasound
    • Colour Doppler scan
    • Contrast-enhanced ultrasound
  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

Mammography

Mammography is a low-dose X-ray imaging technique used for breast assessment. It serves both screening and diagnostic purposes. The standard mammography views include:

  • Craniocaudal (CC)
  • Mediolateral oblique (MLO)

Additional mammography views may be used for a more comprehensive evaluation. These include:

  • Lateral view
  • Exaggerated CC view
  • Cleavage view
  • Magnification view
  • Spot-compression view

Interpretation of a Mammogram

The appearance of a mammogram depends on the amount of glandular and fatty tissue present in the breast. Key points to consider during mammogram interpretation include:

  • In young females or older women on Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT), there is typically more glandular tissue, which appears as soft tissue density on the mammogram.
  • In older women, there is usually more fatty tissue, which appears as less dense or radiolucent on the mammogram.
  • Cooper's ligaments are visible as curved radiopaque lines.
  • The space between the glandular tissue and the chest wall, containing retromammary fat, should appear uniformly radiolucent.
  • The skin is visible as a thin radiopaque line, and the pectoralis muscle appears as a triangular soft tissue shadow.

Breast Ultrasound (USS)

Breast ultrasound is performed using a high-frequency (7.5-13MHz) linear array probe. The patient is in a supine position, with the arm of the breast being examined raised above the head to flatten out the breast and expose the axilla. Various planes, including transverse, longitudinal, radial, and anti-radial, are used for examination.

Appearance
  • The skin is visible as a linear echogenic layer.
  • Subcutaneous tissue appears as a low reflective layer below the skin.
  • Glandular tissue is seen as oval-shaped, circumscribed soft tissue dense bundles, separated by Cooper's ligaments.
  • Posterior to the glandular tissue is the echolucent retromammary fat layer.
  • The pectoralis muscle is enclosed between the linear echogenic superficial and deep pectoralis fascia.
  • The ribs are visible end-on as curved highly reflective structures on the chest wall.

Testes

Imaging Modalities for the Testes

  • Scrotal USS
  • CT
  • MRI
  • RNI
Scrotal Ultrasound (USS)

  • Performed with a high-frequency probe (7.5-10MHz)
  • The testis is an oval, soft tissue-dense organ
  • Measuring 3-5cm in length by 2-3cm in width
  • The epididymis, seen as a coil-like echogenic structure, lies posterior to the testis
  • The testis and epididymis are enclosed by a membrane, the tunica vaginalis, which is lubricated by some fluid

Abnormalities: Testicular Tumors

  • Tumors in the testes are often germ cell in origin, including seminomas, teratomas, embryonal cell tumors, and choriocarcinoma.
  • They are most common in young adults and have a higher incidence in undescended testes.

Ultrasonography (USS)

  • Testicular tumors typically appear as echo-poor lesions with areas of necrosis.
  • Calcifications are common features seen in these tumors.

Hydrocele

  • Hydrocele is seen as anechoic fluid around the testis.
  • It can be congenital or acquired, with causes including trauma (hematoceles), inflammation (pyocele), or malignancy.

Spermatocele & Epididymal Cysts

  • Spermatocele and epididymal cysts present as anechoic lesions within the testis or spermatic cord.

Epididymo-orchitis

  • Epididymo-orchitis is typically a result of urinary tract pathogens.
  • On ultrasound (USS), the epididymis appears enlarged and echo-poor.

Varicocele

  • Varicocele is more commonly observed on the left side, with veins measuring greater than 2mm in diameter.
  • It is visualized as serpiginous, dilated vessels on Doppler ultrasound (USS).

Undescended Testis

  • The ectopic testis can be located anywhere along the route of normal descent from the abdomen to the scrotum, including the groin.
  • It is most commonly found in the inguinal canal (70% of cases).

Trauma

  • Trauma to the testes can result in testicular rupture, scrotal hematoma, or hematoceles.

Testicular Torsion

  • Testicular torsion is an acute emergency that requires investigation within 1 hour to save the testis.
  • Testes affected by torsion appear enlarged and echo-poor.
  • Color Doppler ultrasound can differentiate it from acute epididymo-orchitis.
  • A radioisotope scan will reveal a proton-deficient area surrounded by activity.
