Radiological Methods of Investigating the Heart

  • Chest Radiograph: This common diagnostic tool helps detect chamber enlargement and visualize lung vessel anatomy.
  • Echocardiography (Ultrasound): This non-invasive imaging technique uses sound waves to produce real-time images of the heart. Color Doppler Imaging (CDI) and Power Doppler Ultrasound (PDU) can be added for better visualization of blood flow.
  • Computerized Tomography (CT): CT scans provide detailed cross-sectional images of the heart and surrounding structures.
  • Angiocardiography and Coronary Arteriography: These invasive procedures involve injecting a contrast dye into blood vessels to visualize the heart's chambers and coronary arteries.
  • Phlebography and Lymphography: These procedures use contrast agents to examine veins and lymphatic vessels.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): MRI scans utilize strong magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images of the heart. Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA) is a variant used for vascular assessment.
  • Fluoroscopy: This real-time X-ray imaging technique is often used during interventional procedures to guide medical devices within the heart.
  • Tomography: Tomographic imaging produces detailed cross-sectional images by capturing X-ray images from multiple angles.
  • Radionuclide Studies: These studies involve injecting a radioactive tracer into the bloodstream to assess heart function and blood flow using gamma cameras or PET scanners.

Plain Radiograph Anatomy

  • Plain radiograph is not specific for heart diseases.
  • Demonstrates chamber enlargement.
  • Demonstrates lung vessel anatomy.
  • Demonstrates the bony thorax.

Views for Plain Chest Radiograph

  • PA view (Posterior-Anterior): This is the standard view for chest radiography and is taken with the patient facing the X-ray detector.
  • AP view (Anterior-Posterior): Used for ill patients and for bedside imaging, this view is taken with the X-ray source behind the patient.
  • Right/Left Anterior Oblique: These oblique views are obtained by tilting the patient's body to provide a different perspective of the chest structures.
  • Lateral Decubitus: In this view, the patient lies on their side, which helps evaluate fluid accumulation or air distribution within the chest.

Computed Tomography (CT)

  • CT is a valuable imaging technique for assessing the heart.
  • Modern CT scanners offer high-resolution images of cardiac structures.
  • There are specialized CT techniques used in cardiac imaging:
    • Ultrafast CT: This technique allows for rapid image acquisition, reducing motion artifacts and improving image quality.
    • Helical CT: Helical or spiral CT involves continuous scanning while the patient is moved through the scanner. This technique provides detailed cardiac morphology and can be used to evaluate congenital heart diseases and intracardiac masses.
      • Helical CT can also use contrast enhancement, which improves visualization of blood vessels and cardiac structures.

Magnetic resonance Imaging

  • MRI is a powerful imaging technique used to examine the heart.
  • It offers several advantages for cardiac imaging:
    • Wide Topographical Field of View: MRI provides a broad view of cardiac structures and surrounding tissues.
    • Multiple Imaging Planes: MRI allows for imaging from various angles and planes, enabling a comprehensive assessment of the heart.
    • High Soft Tissue Resolution: MRI provides exceptional soft tissue resolution, allowing differentiation of blood flow from myocardium, even without the use of contrast agents.
    • Non-Ionizing Radiation: Unlike some other imaging methods, MRI uses non-ionizing radiation, making it safer in terms of radiation exposure.
  • However, it's important to note that MRI can be very expensive and may not be readily available in all healthcare facilities.

Cardiac Ultrasound (Echocardiography)

  • Echocardiography is a cardiac imaging technique based on pulse echo technology.
  • It is used to visualize and assess the anatomy and movements of various structures within the heart.
  • There are different modes of echocardiography, each serving specific purposes:
    • 2D Echocardiography: Provides a two-dimensional view of the heart's structures.
    • M-Mode Echocardiography: Displays motion of specific structures over time.
    • Doppler Echocardiography: Measures blood flow velocities and direction within the heart and blood vessels.
    • Color Flow Doppler Echocardiography: Shows the direction and speed of blood flow in color, aiding in the detection of abnormalities.
    • Invasive Echocardiography: Performed during invasive procedures to guide interventions.
    • 3D Echocardiography: Provides a three-dimensional view of the heart's structures for more detailed assessment.
    • Exercise Echocardiography: Utilized during exercise stress testing to assess cardiac function under stress conditions.
  • Echocardiography is a versatile and widely used imaging modality that helps healthcare professionals evaluate cardiac function, diagnose heart conditions, and monitor treatment outcomes.

Chamber Enlargement

Chamber Enlargement: Right Atrium

  • In radiology, chamber enlargement of the right atrium (RA) can be observed on various views of chest radiographs.
  • On the anterior-posterior (AP) view, an enlarged right atrium can contribute to the formation of the right lower heart border.
  • On the lateral view, the posterior wall of the right atrium is marked by the entrance of the inferior vena cava (IVC).
  • An increased size of the right atrium can be identified by specific radiographic features:
    • Increased curvature of the right lower heart border, occupying more than one-third of the cardiac silhouette on the AP view.
    • Enlarged right atrial appendage, often appearing broad-based and capable of filling the retrosternal space inferiorly on the lateral view.

Several cardiac conditions can lead to right atrial enlargement, including:

  • Tricuspid stenosis
  • Tricuspid incompetence
  • Ebsteinâ€™s anomaly
  • Atrial septal defect (ASD)

Chamber Enlargement: Right Ventricle

  • The right ventricle (RV) typically does not significantly contribute to the cardiac outline on the frontal view of chest radiographs.
  • On the lateral view, the right ventricle forms most of the anterior border of the heart.
  • An increase in the size of the right ventricle can lead to specific radiographic features:
    • Bulging of the lower heart border with an upturning of the apex, resulting in a characteristic "boot shape."
    • Increased contact of the anterior heart border with the back of the sternum, especially in the upper part of the lateral view.

Several cardiac conditions can cause right ventricular enlargement, including:

  • Pulmonary hypertension (PH)
  • Pulmonary valve disease
  • Tricuspid valve incompetence
  • Atrial septal defect (ASD)
  • Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
  • Mitral valve stenosis
×

Left Atrial Enlargement

  • The left atrium (LA) typically does not have a significant contribution to the cardiac border on the frontal view of chest radiographs.
  • On the lateral view, the left atrium forms the posterior border of the heart.
  • Its appendage appears narrow and finger-like, creating a double density shadow that crosses to the right side.

Signs of an increase in the left atrium include:

  • Straightening of the upper heart border on the frontal view (known as mitralization) due to the appendage.
  • The left atrium may create a double density shadow that extends from the heart, progressing to form the right heart border (RHB) and, in gross cases, extending to the left heart border (LHB).
  • On the lateral view, the left atrium displaces a barium-filled structure.

Common causes of left atrial enlargement include:

  • Mitral valve stenosis
  • Mitral valve incompetence

Left Ventricular Enlargement

  • On the frontal view of chest radiographs, the left ventricle (LV) forms the left heart border.
  • On the lateral view, the LV forms the lower posterior border of the heart.

Signs of an increase in the left ventricle include:

  • Rounding of the cardiac apex (LVH - Left Ventricular Hypertrophy).
  • Elongation of the cardiac apex to the left and downwards.
  • On the lateral view, the soft tissue shadow of the LV may extend beyond the Barium-filled esophagus or more than 2cm beyond the back of the right atrium (RA).

Common causes of left ventricular enlargement include:

  • Hypertension
  • Coarctation or stenosis of the aorta
  • Mitral stenosis or incompetence
  • Myocardial ischemia or cardiomyopathy
  • Ventricular septal defect (VSD) (Eisenmenger syndrome)
  • Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)

Aortic Enlargement

  • Forms the superior upper left heart border.
  • The ascending part of the aorta is covered by the superior vena cava (SVC) on the right side in the superior mediastinum.

Signs of an increase in the aorta include:

  • Dilated ascending aorta causing a bulge on the right side.
  • The widest curvatures of the aorta should measure between 4-6cm in adults.

Common causes of aortic enlargement include:

  • Aortic incompetence
  • Aortic stenosis
  • Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
  • Atherosclerotic aorta
  • Aneurysms (such as syphilitic or dissecting aneurysms)

Pulmonary Artery

  • Forms the upper left heart border between the aortic arch and the heart proper.
  • Usually forms a pulmonary bay, which is typically straight.
    • Increased curvature or convexity of the pulmonary bay.

Common causes of changes in the pulmonary artery shape include:

  • Pulmonary heart disease (pulmonary hypertension)
  • Mitral valve disease
  • Congenital heart diseases such as ventricular septal defects (VSD), atrial septal defects (ASD), and patent ductus arteriosus (PDA).

The Pericardium

  • Consists of two layers: parietal and visceral, which enclose the heart and are separated by a potential space lubricated by pericardial fluid.
  • Usually not demonstrated radiologically, as it has the same radiological density as the heart.
  • It may become visible if a significant amount of fat is present, appearing as a low-density shadow with the pericardium showing as a thin white line on the outside.

Pericardial Effusion

  • The most common abnormality of the pericardium seen radiologically.
  • Radiographic Findings:
    • Globular shape or nonspecific heart enlargement.
    • This increase in heart size can change in serial films.
    • There is no congestion of the pulmonary vasculature due to obstruction of venous return.
  • Other investigations to confirm pericardial effusion include:
    • Echocardiography
    • CT (Computerized Tomography)
  • Common Causes:
    • Inflammatory Causes:
      • Tuberculous
      • Bacterial
      • Viral
    • Heart Diseases:
      • Cardiac failure
      • Myocardial infarction
    • Malignant Diseases:
      • Usually metastases (e.g., breast cancer)
      • Lymphoma
    • Endocrine Diseases:
      • Myxedema
    • Other Conditions:
      • Uremia
      • Collagen Diseases, such as systemic lupus erythematosus

Constrictive Pericarditis

  • Constrictive pericarditis is a condition where there's impairment of filling of the heart chambers, most often affecting the right heart due to thickening and rigidity of the pericardium.
  • Common Causes:
    • Viral infections
    • Bacterial infections
    • Haemopericardium (blood accumulation in the pericardial sac)
    • Collagen vascular diseases
  • Radiographic Findings:
    • Calcification seen in about 50% of cases.
    • Straightening of the right heart border from superior vena cava (SVC) to inferior vena cava (IVC).
    • The heart borders may be roughened due to pleuropericardial adhesions.

Pericardial Tumors (Rare)

  • The common pericardial tumor is a benign pleuropericardial cyst.
  • Other potential causes include metastatic diseases.

Heart Failure

Radiographic Findings of Heart Failure

  • Left-Sided Heart Failure
    • Upper lobe veins distension.
    • Indistinct or full hilum indicating early pulmonary edema.
    • Interstitial edema, characterized by:
      • Kerley A lines: Long, fine lines radiating from the hilum to the periphery in upper lobes.
      • Kerley B lines: Short, fine non-branching lines at the periphery of lower lobes above the costophrenic sulci.
    • Alveolar pulmonary edema, seen as diffuse reduction in pulmonary translucency spreading from hilum peripherally.
    • Pulmonary edema, usually seen as localized, homogenous opacity, especially in the apices.
  • Right Ventricular Failure
    • Enlarged superior vena cava (SVC).
    • Enlarged azygous vein shadow (>2 cm).
    • Pleural effusion, usually bilateral but right-sided greater than left.
    • Interlobular or fissural effusion, seen as oval opacity in fissures which clears with treatment (vanishing lung tumor).
    • Pericardial effusion.

Pulmonary Hypertension

  • Prominence of the left heart border (LHB) between the aortic and left atrial appendage contour, indicating an enlarged main pulmonary artery.
  • Enlargement of the end-on pulmonary artery branch, seen to be bigger than the adjacent bronchus.
  • Prominence of the second impression on a barium-filled esophagus due to pressure from the bronchus.
  • Enlargement of upper lobe veins with constriction of the lower lobe veins due to increased pulmonary venous pressure.
  • Constriction of peripheral pulmonary arteries.
  • Causes of Pulmonary Hypertension
    1. Pulmonary Disease
      • Fibrotic pulmonary diseases: Tuberculosis (Tb), sarcoidosis, bronchiectasis, interstitial diseases, etc.
      • Obstructive pulmonary diseases: Chronic bronchiectasis, multiple pulmonary emboli.
      • Idiopathic pulmonary arteriosclerotic diseases, including Ayerza's syndrome.
    2. Mitral Valve Disease
    3. Congenital Heart Disease
      • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)
      • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)
      • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
      • Pulmonary valve stenosis with associated post-stenotic dilatation.

Systemic Hypertension

  • Causes of Systemic Hypertension
    1. Idiopathic
    2. Familial
    3. Renal Causes
      • Pyelonephritis
      • Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)
      • Renal Artery Stenosis (RA Stenosis)
    4. Pituitary-Adrenal Axis Causes
      • Pheochromocytoma
      • Adrenal Hyperplasia
      • Conn's Disease (Primary Aldosteronism)
      • Cushing's Syndrome
    5. Coarctation of the Aorta
  • Radiological Investigations for Hypertension
    1. Chest X-ray (CXR)
    2. Abdominal X-ray/Ultrasound (USS)
    3. Intravenous Urography (IVU)
    4. Skull X-ray
    5. Angiogram (Aortography)
    6. Computed Tomography (CT)
    7. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Radiographic Features in the Chest for Hypertension
    1. Features of Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)
      • Rounding of the cardiac apex
      • Downward bulge of the lower left heart border (LHB)
      • Unfolding of the aorta
      • Prominent aortic knuckle
    2. Features of Left Ventricular Failure (LV Failure)
      • Prominent upper lobe veins
      • Septal lines
      • Pulmonary edema
    3. Features of Right Ventricular Failure (RV Failure)
      • Prominent Superior Vena Cava (SVC)
      • Enlarged azygous vein
      • Pleural effusion

Congenital Heart Diseases

    1. Left-Right Shunts (Acyanotic CHD)
      • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)
      • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)
      • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
      • Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Drainage (APVD)
    2. Right-Left Shunts (Cyanotic CHD)
      • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF): Pulmonary stenosis (RV outlet obstruction) + Overriding aorta + VSD + RVH
        • If ASD is present, it's called Fallot's Pentalogy
      • Truncus Arteriosus
      • Eisenmenger's Complex

    3. Abnormality of Position
      • Dextrocardia of the heart
      • Dextrocardia of the Aorta
      • Dextrocardia of the Superior Vena Cava (SVC)
      • Dextrocardia of the Pulmonary Artery
    4. Abnormality of Size
      • Increase in size
        • Glycogen Storage Disease
        • Endocardial Fibroelastosis
      • Decrease in size
        • Coarctation of the Aorta
        • Pulmonary Stenosis
        • Pulmonary Atresia (Blood flow through PDA or bronchial arteries)
        • Ebstein's Anomaly
    5. Pericardial Defect

Radiological Terminologies

  • Dextrocardia: Malposition of the heart or great vessels.
  • Rib Notching: Indentation of the inner, inferior aspect of the ribs due to collateral circulation resulting from coarctation of the aorta. Differential Diagnosis (DD) includes Neurofibromatosis, obstructed SVC, and Post-Blalock operation.
  • Unilateral Hypertransradiancy (Macleod's Syndrome): Seen in atresia of a branch of the pulmonary artery.
  • Cottage-Loaf Appearance: Seen in Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Drainage (APVD).
  • Boot-Shape Heart: Seen in Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH) and Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF).
  • Shelf-Like Left Ventricle: Seen in Truncus Arteriosus.
